    <div className="annotation-text-holder">
      <h2>1980: Redesigning the “Crossroads of the World”</h2>
      <p>In November 1980, the Urban Mass Transportation Administration (UMTA) and the City of New York released an Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) proposing a radical transformation of Broadway through Times Square. 
      The plan sought to close sections of Broadway to private vehicles and convert four blocks into a pedestrian and transit mall. Amenities such as trees, monuments, information kiosks, seating and bicycle lanes would replace existing traffic lanes, 
      while buses and taxis would receive priority access through dedicated lay-bys.</p>
    </div>
</div>

<div className="annotation full-width sticky-annotation">
    <div className="photo-container">
      <img src="/images/from-cars-to-crowds/Image_2_1975_Times_Square_Getty_Image.webp"
        alt="Times Square in 1975." 
        className="anno-photo" />
    </div>
    <div className="annotation-text-holder">
      <p>At a time when Times Square was associated wth congestion, street crime and economic decline, the proposal framed this transformation as a critical investment in the city’s rejuvenation. The document argued that the “Broadway Plaza” would:</p>
      <ul>
      <li>Expand pedestrian space for an estimated 500,000 daily visitors</li>
      <li>Improve surface transit reliability and convenience</li>
      <li>Enhance safety by eliminating dangerous intersections</li>
      <li>Stimulate private investment, including a proposed $240 million, 2,000-room hotel</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
</div>

<div className="annotation full-width sticky-annotation">
    <div className="photo-container">

    <div className="annotation-text-holder">
      <p>However, the EIS also acknowledged that the redesign would create significant adverse effects. It noted that the project would:</p>
      <ul>
      <li>Shift traffic to surrounding avenues</li>
      <li>Increase carbon monoxide levels and noise in adjacent corridors</li>
      <li>Disrupt local businesses during an estimated two-and-a-half-year construction period</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
</div>
