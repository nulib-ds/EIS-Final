            The history of urban renewal in the United States, as seen through these sources from the late 19th century to the early 1970s, reveals a consistent narrative of tension between modernization and community identity.
            While the term "gentrification" is rarely used in these 1970s documents, the mechanics—displacement of low-income populations, the demolition of "blighted" structures, and the push for higher-value commercial and residential use—form its foundational story.
           </p>
           <p>
            A major point of conflict in these stories across the U.S. is the struggle to define what is "historic" and worth saving.
